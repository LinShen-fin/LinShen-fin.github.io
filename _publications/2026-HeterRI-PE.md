Information efficiency is a core market quality metric that reflects how well securities markets aggregate private information from market participants, facilitate price discovery, and ultimately enhance capital allocation efficiency in the real economy. Existing research indicates that retail investor-dominated market structure of China significantly weakens information efficiency, presenting regulatory authorities with the institutional challenge of balancing investor protection and market quality improvement. This study examines retail investor heterogeneity by adopting the method proposed by Xiong et al. (2024) to explore how different types of retail investors differentially influence market information efficiency and, consequently, real-economy resource allocation performance. Empirical findings reveal that "trend-chasing" investors significantly enhance the valuation informativeness of their held assets: a one-standard-deviation increase in their shareholding proportion leads to a 16.4% improvement in the future earnings information embedded in valuations. This improvement generates a dual effect on resource allocation: at the firm level, it encourages managers to adjust investment decisions based on price, while at the market level, it directs capital toward more efficient sectors. These allocation improvements ultimately translate into real economic output: firms held by "trend-chasing" investors exhibit higher future innovation output and total factor productivity. Furthermore, through discontinuity regression, robustness checks, and alternative hypotheses tests, this study establishes a causal relationship between "trend-chasing" investor ownership and improved information efficiency, identifying information discovery as the primary channel driving this effect. Beyond contributing new empirical evidence to heterogeneous investor theory, this study constructs an investor suitability management optimization model from a regulatory perspective, offering a solution with both theoretical depth and practical value for establishing tiered supervision mechanisms and advancing capital market reform.
