This paper develops a non-linear rational expectation equilibrium to examine how market liquidity is affected by the trading of "endogenous liquidity providers" (ELPs) who switch between limit and market orders endogenously based on their private signals. ELPs trade off gains from trading against noise traders (through limit orders) versus on private information (through market orders). When observing extreme private information, ELPs switch from limit to market orders. The switching exacerbates the adverse selection of designated market makers (DMMs), particularly with balanced aggregate order flows. Therefore, DMMs' inference of the risky payoff becomes concave to order flows. When trading through limit orders, ELPs effectively reinforce the liquidity provision of DMMs, improving market liquidity. However, with unexpected shocks, ELPs withdraw limit orders and switch to market orders, generating liquidity gaps and hence liquidity fragility and market breaks.
