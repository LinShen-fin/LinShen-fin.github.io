The REE literature suggests that exploring noise demand helps investors infer the fundamental value embedded in asset prices. However, both noise exploration and fundamental value exploration lead investors to bet against dumb money (BADM) and earn excess returns, which makes it difficult to empirically prove the existence of noise-exploring ability. This paper distinguishes between the two abilities in terms of their behavioral and economic outcomes by proposing an REE model in which agents possess both noise-exploring and fundamental-exploring abilities. Three testable predictions emerge: 1) Investor behavior, in terms of BADM, is more strongly correlated with noise-exploring ability than performance; 2) Noise-exploring ability, captured by BADM, provides additional predictive power for future returns beyond historical performance; 3) Since noise-exploring ability works by filtering out noise in price information, when prices are excessively noisy (illiquid), noise-exploring ability becomes less effective, prompting investors to rely more on fundamental-exploring ability, thereby weakening the correlation between noise-exploring and BADM behavior. We test these predictions and confirm the existence of noise-exploring ability by examining the BADM behaviors and their impact on the performance of mutual funds, using a unique measure of dumb money in China.
